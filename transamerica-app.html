Transamerica App:
Designing a Mobile App that Empowers Users on their Retirement Journey

Transamerica is a financial services company that helps millions of people prepare for retirement through investment products and workplace retirement plans. Their focus is on making financial planning more accessible, building trust through education, and supporting people in living healthier, wealthier lives*.

The problem, in detail

Transamerica has two retirement journey management mobile apps: one app places a focus on Wealth and Health but lacks the functionality the users need (more recent); the other app meets the users‚Äô needs but does not embrace the Wealth and Health philosophy (legacy). We needed to merge the two into one. I asked myself:

  • How could we design a mobile app that embodies the Wealth and Health philosophy and provide key retirement journey management functionality?
  • How could we create a space that could engage the users beyond the simple task of checking account balances?
  • How could we add, improve, or augment 21 features in a design deliverable timeline of six months?

One of our biggest design challenges was working within those constraints and minimizing any new patterns or styles; we had to stick with what was in the more recent app and build on that. We had a small yet very effective team of three designers and one researcher.

Discovery

Our initial discovery phase shed light on key issues we needed to address:

  • The user base of the more recent app was upset about the lack of financial tools that were available in the legacy app.
  • The users of the more recent app were confused: Why was Transamerica requiring users to answer Health questions when they just wanted to check their account status? What does Health have to do with Wealth?
  • The user base of the more recent app reverted to the legacy app as that offered more functionality, even though the experience and visuals were outdated.

Solution: Navigation & IA

One of the major challenges was navigation: the app IA had to be redefined. We needed to:

  • Place emphasis on financial features
  • Make room for a lot of new content (2x the features)
  • Allow user to focus on one plan at a time

Paired designing was key at this phase. We created a lot of mockups and finally ended on three main nav items: Overview, Manage, More.

I mocked up navigation variations

The final solution for the main navigation was driven by our IA restructure. We proposed that the three most important items for the user would allow them to review their accounts at a high-level and then to manage them.

Other features such as the Health + Wealth content would fit under the ‚ÄúKnowledge center‚Äù under More. This would serve as an alternate home for educational content.

Solution: Complex flows

Complex features required a large effort on analysis. We created logic charts and feature option matrixes to help us design the features.

Complex features such as Contributions and Allocations were crucial tools for the user. In order to create an experience for users that was simple and consumable, placing Guidance touchpoints and simplifying content organization was key.

I simplified the app experience by focusing on the UI

Test & learn

Our team conducted usability testing on the most complex features, which included navigation. We created three robust, clickable prototypes and synthesized the findings as a group.

A big thing we learned from research was that retirement in general is confusing. Users need more guidance than we were giving them. Our team then created a strategy to deliver more Help content to the app without pushing out our deliverable date.

Style guides

Our team created style guides for the merged app due to updated brand and component styles based on the accessibility audit. A few things that changed to meet AA standards:

  • Font size
  • Button styling and size
  • Selection control styling and sizes
  • Data visual colors and format
